# Enterprise Developer Friction in the Age of AI Coding Agents

## Research lens

This review prioritizes observed engineering behavior over vendor narratives or self-reported wish lists. The evidence base combines large-scale field studies of developer work, peer-reviewed software engineering research on comprehension, bug fixing, architecture and change impact, enterprise studies of AI coding assistant use, DORA research on software delivery, and official platform/tool documentation from GitHub, Backstage, ServiceNow, Atlassian, and AWS. The goal is to isolate persistent, high-cost workflow failures rather than validate any preselected product category. ## What daily work actually looks like

The daily workflow of enterprise developers is best described as repeated context reconstruction. Developers move between code, tickets, docs, dashboards, logs, chat, review tools, and people to answer practical questions such as: what is actually running, who owns it, what changed recently, what constraints apply here, what else depends on this, and how risky is a change. The comprehension study found that developers frequently switch among IDEs, browsers, and document editors, and eight of ten interviewees said that this switching hurts productivity because they lose their place and must mentally recover context. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn17view0Ņłéturn17view18Ņłü

Enterprise AI assistant usage reinforces that picture. In IBMŌĆÖs internal watsonx Code Assistant study, the most common use cases were code explanation and answers to programming questions, not raw code generation; 71.9% of respondents used it to explain code and 68.5% to answer general programming questions. That is a strong signal that the dominant unsolved problem is still understanding existing systems, not typing new code faster. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn29view1Ņłü

The workflow differences across common change types are more about missing context than about lack of editing speed. ## How AI changes the workflow

AI assistants are now mainstream in developer workflows, but their current role is narrower and more conditional than marketing often implies. JetBrains reported in 2024 that 69% of developers had tried ChatGPT for coding-related tasks and 49% used it regularly; GitHub Copilot had been tried by 40% and used regularly by 26%. Stack OverflowŌĆÖs 2025 survey found similarly broad adoption of AI tooling, but also rising distrust: 46% of developers distrust the accuracy of AI output, versus 33% who trust it. About 35% of developers said at least some of their visits to Stack Overflow were caused by AI-related issues, which means human-verified knowledge is being used as a repair channel for AI-assisted work. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn11view7Ņłéturn14view1Ņłéturn14view0Ņłü

DORAŌĆÖs findings explain why enterprise teams feel both faster and less safe. AI adoption increases individual productivity, flow, and job satisfaction, but a 25% increase in AI adoption is associated with a 1.5% decrease in delivery throughput and a 7.2% decrease in delivery stability. DORA explicitly ties this to larger batch sizes and review burden. In a separate DORA note on AI tensions, the burden shift is described clearly: the author can generate a large pull request quickly, but the reviewer still has to manually inspect every line. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn11view0Ņłéturn11view1Ņłéturn11view2Ņłü

The enterprise studies and official GitHub guidance show that developers compensate for this by manually providing more structure. GitHub recommends that cloud-agent tasks be well scoped, with a clear problem description, complete acceptance criteria, and hints about what files should change; it also recommends custom instructions so the agent better understands the project and how to build, test, and validate changes. The empirical study of developer-authored rule files found 401 repositories containing these project-level AI instructions. Developers were not just adding generic preferences; they were encoding conventions, guidelines, project information, examples, and explicit LLM directives because the default assistant lacked that context. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn25view3Ņłéturn26view0Ņłü

That allows a useful separation between temporary model limitations and structural organizational problems. Temporary limitations include inaccurate or incomplete outputs, weak performance on complex or novel tasks, uneven speed, and ambiguity handling. Structural problems include missing acceptance criteria, weak tests, stale or scattered documentation, hidden service dependencies, unclear ownership, repository or policy access restrictions, and delivery processes that still require manual approvals and coordination. DORAŌĆÖs 2025 AI report states this directly: successful AI adoption is a systems problem, not a tools problem. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn25view3Ņłéturn29view5Ņłéturn24view2Ņłéturn36view0Ņłü

## Why current tool categories still miss

Developer portals and service catalogs help with navigation, but they are not a live model of reality. AWS presents Backstage-style portals as a unifying layer for capabilities such as CI/CD, observability, security, tenancy, and service catalogs. Backstage itself documents that catalog metadata lives in YAML files in source control and that the catalog should not be considered the ultimate source of truth; it is better treated as a cache over underlying systems. That is useful for discoverability, but it means the portal inherits a freshness and maintenance problem exactly where developers need trustworthy, up-to-date answers. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn34view0Ņłéturn34view1Ņłéturn35view0Ņłéturn11view10Ņłéturn11view11Ņłü

CMDBs solve a different problem than developers usually have. ServiceNowŌĆÖs CMDB health model explicitly tracks correctness, completeness, and compliance, and its data-management tooling includes stale-data cleanup because referenced configuration items drift over time. That is a sign of reality, not a flaw in ServiceNow: inventory systems are hard to keep current. But it also means CMDBs are optimized for record quality and governance, not for developer decision support inside a change, review, or incident workflow. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn11view12Ņłéturn11view13Ņłéturn4search15Ņłü

Documentation platforms and internal search remain necessary but insufficient. DORA found that documentation quality is foundational and linked to organizational performance, yet Atlassian still reports that ŌĆ£finding informationŌĆØ is the top developer time-waster. In other words, having docs matters, but ŌĆ£docs existŌĆØ is not the same as ŌĆ£the right answer is available, current, and trusted in the moment of change.ŌĆØ The ADR case study makes the same point at a smaller scale: teams already had wiki pages and visualizations, but lack of centralization and accessibility still caused confusion and slowed decision-making. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn11view4Ņłéturn11view5Ņłéturn15view0Ņłéturn30view0Ņłü

IDE tooling and code-hosting workflows are also narrower than the problem space. GitHubŌĆÖs CODEOWNERS is path-based and file-scoped; GitHub documents that CODEOWNERS files above 3 MB are ignored, while BitbucketŌĆÖs equivalent notes that fragmented expertise makes finding the right reviewers difficult and caps automatically added reviewers at 100. GitHubŌĆÖs own code-referencing documentation says the underlying search index may be refreshed only every few months, so newly committed or moved code may be missing or stale in results. These are reasonable product constraints, but they leave large gaps around freshness, cross-repository reasoning, runtime ownership, and architectural context. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn11view14Ņłéturn20view0Ņłéturn11view15Ņłéturn11view16Ņłü

## The most severe unsolved problems

### Trustworthy engineering context is fragmented and expires quickly

**Problem.** The day-to-day pain is not ŌĆ£I need more code generated.ŌĆØ It is ŌĆ£I cannot confidently reconstruct what this system is, how it works now, what rules apply here, and which information source I should trust.ŌĆØ Developers, platform engineers, and engineering managers all experience this, but it hits individual contributors and reviewers most often because it appears inside almost every non-trivial task. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn15view0Ņłéturn17view0Ņłéturn17view18Ņłéturn11view6Ņłü

**Current behavior.** People search across browsers, docs, code, tickets, and chat; they ask teammates; they use AI to explain code; and increasingly they write project-level AI instructions to encode local conventions. IBMŌĆÖs enterprise study found that code understanding was the top AI use case, and the rule-file study found developers explicitly authoring context about conventions, guidelines, project information, and examples because assistants need it to be useful. **Root cause.** This is partly a data problem and partly a workflow problem. The relevant knowledge is distributed across code, build/test systems, runbooks, tickets, architecture notes, and people. ### Change impact analysis is still mostly manual guesswork

**Problem.** For feature work, refactors, migrations, and production fixes, the hardest question is often not how to edit the code, but how to estimate the blast radius before making the change. Developers need to know what other services, schemas, configs, jobs, or consumers will be affected, including indirect or semantic coupling. This occurs whenever a change crosses a repository boundary, touches shared logic, or affects distributed behavior. The impact is high because wrong guesses create oversized PRs, escaped defects, rollback risk, migration delays, and political resistance to change. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn23search0Ņłéturn23search20Ņłéturn32view1Ņłü

**Current behavior.** Teams grep across repositories, inspect static dependencies, read diagrams, check recent commits, ask experts, and rely on staging, canaries, or production signals to validate assumptions late. In more mature setups they consult service catalogs, tracing data, or ADRs, but those are usually partial views stitched together manually. Reviewers then act as human impact-analysis engines, which breaks down when change sets are large. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn11view11Ņłéturn30view0Ņłéturn32view2Ņłéturn11view2Ņłü

**Root cause.** The root cause is a mix of technical and organizational coupling. DORAŌĆÖs loosely coupled teams guidance says teams perform best when they can change, test, and deploy without depending on other teams; it also warns that in tightly coupled architectures, small changes can cause cascading failures and force constant coordination. Research on microservice change impact echoes that: changes can propagate through direct and indirect dependencies, and the literature still lacks an active mitigation instrument for ripple effects across the whole system. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn32view1Ņłéturn23search0Ņłéturn23search20Ņłü

**Existing alternatives.** Catalogs and CMDBs mostly answer ŌĆ£what existsŌĆØ rather than ŌĆ£what is likely to break if this specific diff lands.ŌĆØ Static dependency tools miss runtime relationships and semantic duplication. **Evidence.** The 2025 systematic review on microservice change impact says changes in one microservice can create ripple effects and extended maintenance effort, even without a direct connection. **Opportunity assessment.** Yes, especially for organizations with many services, shared platforms, or legacy-modernization programs. **Problem.** Enterprise teams still lose time on ŌĆ£who owns this?ŌĆØ and ŌĆ£who needs to approve or help with this?ŌĆØ even though CODEOWNERS, service catalogs, on-call systems, and org charts all exist. ### Verification has become the hidden cost center of AI-assisted development

**Current behavior.** Developers treat AI like a junior teammate or intern: useful, fast, but requiring supervision.
